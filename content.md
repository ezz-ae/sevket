The Sevet Ölmez Project outlines a sophisticated restaurant business infrastructure designed to transform the traditional food service model into a measurable, repeatable, and scalable asset class. At the center of this ecosystem is SevetTeam, a parent company founded by Turkish-British entrepreneur Sevet Ölmez (Şevketullah Ölmez). Unlike traditional restaurant companies that focus on culinary flair, SevetTeam functions as a "restaurant business company," prioritizing operational control, unit economics, and data-driven management.

The project’s signature strategy involves placing compact shawarma franchise units inside U.S. gas stations to leverage existing traffic rather than attempting to generate new demand. These units are managed through AFFAREM, a proprietary control system that integrates remote monitoring, smart CCTV, and a unique "Smart Discipline Score" to ensure accountability. With a diverse entry ladder ranging from $1,000 micro-starts to $245,000 premium units, the project aims to turn food service into a disciplined system where every branch must justify its existence through financial transparency and capital recovery.

Founder Profile: Sevet Ölmez

The business philosophy of SevetTeam is deeply rooted in the personal history and psychological framework of its founder.

* Biographical Background: Born Şevketullah Ölmez on October 12, 1988, in Ankara, Turkey. He was raised primarily by a disciplined and practical Turkish father after his English mother returned to London during his early childhood. He eventually moved to England for his education and currently resides in Edinburgh, Scotland.
* Identity and Name Logic:
  * Şevketullah Ölmez: His official legal name, representing his Turkish roots and familial heritage.
  * Şevket Ölmez: His professional business name.
  * Sevet Ölmez: The name used by his UK circle and university peers. This name represents his independent identity and adaptation to the British market.
* Core Philosophy: Driven by a childhood realization that "people leave but systems stay," Ölmez views repetition as the ultimate form of safety and control. His mantra is: "I don’t build restaurants. I build repetition."
* Professional Persona: Known as "The Franchise Architect," he is characterized as a calm, strategic, and emotionally guarded operator who values numbers over "hype" and operational discipline over creative chaos.

The SevetTeam Ecosystem

SevetTeam is structured into three distinct layers that balance brand authority with high-yield scalability.

1. Full Turkish Restaurants (Brand Authority)

Located in major global hubs (London, Edinburgh, Istanbul, New York, etc.), these flagship establishments build cultural trust and serve as training hubs. They establish the brand's culinary credibility and host potential investors.

2. Gas Station Shawarma Units (Core Assets)

This is the primary scalable product. These compact units are placed inside high-traffic U.S. gas stations.

* The Logic: Gas stations already sell movement and have built-in traffic. SevetTeam places food where customers are already pausing.
* The Menu: A strictly limited selection (chicken/beef shawarma, wraps, bowls, and fries) to ensure speed, quality control, and minimal waste.

3. Micro-Start Program (Human Development)

An entry-level tier allowing individuals to start small food businesses with limited capital (1,000–2,000). This program functions as a ladder, allowing operators to grow from street stands to kiosks and eventually full franchise units.

Operational Strategy Framework: Transforming High-Traffic Infrastructure into Managed Food Assets

1. The Foundation: From Restaurant Company to Business Infrastructure

The strategic evolution from a "restaurant company" to a "restaurant business company" represents the core of the Sevet Ölmez methodology. While a traditional restaurant company is preoccupied with the craftsmanship of food, a restaurant business company prioritizes the engineering of the operating interface. This distinction is critical for scalability and risk mitigation; it removes the "genius requirement" from the unit level and replaces it with repeatable business infrastructure. Success in this model is not an emotional mood or a culinary accident; it is the inevitable output of a disciplined machine. As the Franchise Architect asserts: "I don’t build restaurants. I build repetition."

Traditional Food-Service Model	SevetTeam "Operating Interface" Model
Sells food and "atmosphere"	Sells controlled, managed food assets
Depends on specialized culinary talent	Designed for ordinary, disciplined operators
High complexity; "Menu Inflation"	Radical simplicity; "Small Unit Doctrine"
Passive ownership / "Ghost Investors"	Mandatory operational accountability
Success is a culinary opinion	Success is a measurable AFFAREM report

The execution of this vision is decentralized across three specific layers:

* SevetTeam: The parent operating interface that designs the systems and manages the global brand.
* AFFAREM: The proprietary central control layer for real-time monitoring of branches, performance metrics, and investor discipline.
* Sevet Global Market: The investment marketplace where validated franchise assets and territories are structured for deployment.

Credibility for this model is anchored in the 42 British Franchise Holders who, having gained extensive operational experience in the "Istanbul rhythm," now apply British systemic discipline to SevetTeam units. This philosophical foundation necessitates a move away from high-street vanity toward a location strategy based on pre-existing customer movement.

2. Strategic Infrastructure: The Gas Station Synergy Model

The most expensive failure in the food industry is the cost of customer acquisition. Traditional restaurants must spend heavily to manufacture traffic. The Sevet Ölmez methodology bypasses this cost by integrating into existing high-traffic infrastructure: the U.S. gas station network. By placing food assets inside the path of inevitable movement, the model sells hunger to a customer who has already stopped for fuel.

Key differentiators of the U.S. gas station model include:

* Built-in Traffic: Captures impulse and routine behavior without independent marketing spend.
* 24/7 Movement: Provides a constant cycle of day-parts (breakfast, lunch, dinner, late-night) for revenue generation.
* "Hot Food Inside Repeated Movement" Logic: Gas stations sell movement; SevetTeam sells the high-margin hot-food transaction inside that movement.

This strategy is validated by data from the National Association of Convenience Stores (NACS). As of December 31, 2024, there were 121,852 convenience stores in the U.S. selling fuel, handling 80% of the country’s fuel purchases. Crucially, foodservice has become the primary profit engine for these sites, accounting for 27.7% of in-store sales and 38.6% of in-store gross margin dollars. By utilizing this infrastructure, a unit becomes a high-performance micro-asset within a proven engine, necessitating a "Small Unit Doctrine" to fit this specific footprint.

3. The Small Unit Doctrine: Engineering the Repeatable Food Engine

Complexity is the enemy of scale. The "Small Unit Doctrine" dictates that menu complexity must be purged to ensure that ordinary people can repeat the process without the system collapsing. The standardized gas station shawarma unit is designed as the "smallest testable unit" of the SevetTeam engine. Every item added to the menu is a potential point of failure.

The core unit focuses on a surgical menu: Chicken and Beef/Lamb Shawarma (Wraps and Bowls), standardized sides, and combo boxes. To prevent "menu drift," every item must pass Sevet’s rigorous expansion test:

The Four Tests for Menu Expansion:

1. Can it be prepared fast?
2. Can staff learn it quickly?
3. Can ingredients be strictly controlled?
4. Can the profit margin survive potential waste?

This doctrine also creates an entry-level pipeline via the "Micro-Start" tier. These models, costing between $1,000 and $2,000, allow ordinary people to enter the system as operators of small stands or carts. This is the first rung on the ladder; it identifies disciplined talent before they graduate to full-scale gas station units. As Sevet insists: "Never scale confusion." Physical units, however, are useless without the digital nervous system of the AFFAREM control layer.

4. Operational Governance: The AFFAREM Control Layer

In a decentralized network, remote monitoring is the primary governance mechanism. AFFAREM transforms the business from "passive fantasy" to "active intelligence," providing a real-time Operating Room for every asset.

The system is organized into functional "Rooms" tracking specific data points:

1. Investor Room: Investment status, capital requirements, and administrative compliance.
2. Design Room: Blueprints, equipment layouts, and mandatory "Shop Design Sessions" for every unit.
3. Operator Room: Daily shift logs, staff attendance, prep checklists, and speed-of-service.
4. Accountant Room: Financial truth—gross sales, food costs, payroll, and cash variances.
5. LiveOps Room: Management of active interventions for underperforming branches.
6. Payback Room: The "Brutal Number"—capital recovered versus capital remaining.

Because "money is becoming normal but discipline is rare," AFFAREM utilizes the Smart Discipline Score to judge accounts. This behavioral metric ensures that investors and operators are measured by their adherence to the system, not their bank balance.

* Managed Path: The investor hires a professional, Sevet-trained manager to handle their shift responsibility.
